Sum of All Breakers Method

NEC 705.12(B)(2)(3)(b) — An alternative to the 120% rule. The total of all OCPD ratings on the busbar, including the proposed backfeed breaker, must not exceed the busbar rating. No 120% multiplier applies.

  • Panel fails the 120% rule but total load OCPD is low — the sum method may pass without a panel upgrade.
  • AHJ prefers total load enumeration over the percentage-based method.
  • Multiple interactive sources (PV + ESS + generator) where aggregate OCPD is easier to document.
  • Confirming a passing 120% result via an alternate method for stronger permit documentation.
Interpretation varies by AHJ. Confirm acceptance before designing to this method. Some utilities apply more restrictive rules than the NEC minimum.

Back-Fed Breaker & Labeling Requirements
NEC 705.12(B)(3) Backfeed breaker must be secured in position with a listed handle lock or breaker lock. Identified as an interactive system supply connection.
NEC 690.54 Point of interconnection must be labeled with the maximum permitted backfeed current and system voltage. Label must be permanent, weatherproof if outdoors.
NEC 690.31(G)(3) All PV system wiring — junction boxes, conduit, raceways, combiners — must be labeled: WARNING: PHOTOVOLTAIC POWER SOURCE at accessible points.
From the Field — Labeling is the most common reason solar inspections fail on the electrical side. Breaker lock, label size, placement, and exact wording are each individually checked. If multiple inverters are installed, each point of connection requires its own independent label set. Confirm local AHJ label format and size requirements before scheduling final inspection.

Utility Interconnection Agreement May Override NEC

NEC 705.12 sets the minimum code standard for load-side interconnection. Utility Interconnection Agreements (IA) and tariff schedules are contractual and often impose stricter limits — they take precedence in practice regardless of NEC compliance.

Common utility restrictions that override NEC minimums:
  • Export capacity cap lower than NEC maximum (e.g., utility limits export to 15A regardless of panel capacity)
  • Anti-islanding delay requirements beyond IEEE 1547 minimums
  • Prohibition on specific inverter types, firmware versions, or rapid shutdown configurations
  • Metering requirements that restrict installation location or service entrance disconnect position
  • NEM/NEM3 queue restrictions that affect when a system can be energized
  • Minimum interconnection study thresholds that trigger additional engineering review
Always obtain and review the utility IA before submitting permit documents. AHJ approval does not substitute for utility approval — both are required.
