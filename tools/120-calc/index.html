Supply-Side Connection

Supply-side connection bypasses NEC 705.12. No busbar percentage limit applies. Governed by NEC 705.12(A) and 230.82(6).

Supply-side connections are still subject to the following hard limits:

Conductors

Tap conductors must be sized to carry full inverter output current without exceeding ampacity per NEC 310.12. Conductor ampacity must meet or exceed inverter maximum output current × 125% continuous load factor per NEC 705.12(A). Conductor length and conduit fill still apply per NEC 310 and Chapter 9.

Disconnect & Overcurrent Protection

A supply-side disconnect is required per NEC 230.82(6). OCPD must be sized to protect the conductors — not just the inverter output. Service equipment ratings cannot be exceeded. The supply-side connection point must be rated for the combined current.

Service Entrance Equipment

Meter base, service entrance conductors, and all equipment between the utility connection and tap point must be rated for the combined load. Many utilities require written approval and may specify their own sizing requirements independent of NEC. Some utilities prohibit supply-side connections entirely — confirm with AHJ and utility interconnection agreement before designing.

From the Field — Supply-side connections are often chosen when a panel fails the 120% calc. They are not a free pass — they shift sizing constraints from the busbar to the service entrance equipment and conductors. Confirm all component ratings before finalizing design.

Option 1 — Verify Backfeed Breaker Position

Before assuming a full fail, confirm breaker placement. Back-fed breaker must be at the opposite end of the busbar from the main breaker per NEC 705.12(B)(3) [2023] / 705.12(D)(3) [pre-2020]. If placement is the only issue, this is an install correction — not a panel failure.

Option 2 — Reduce System Size

If available backfeed is positive but insufficient for the proposed inverter output, reducing system size may resolve without panel work. Recalculate with a smaller inverter output current.

Option 3 — Supply-Side Connection

Bypasses NEC 705.12 busbar calculation entirely. Governed by NEC 705.12(A) and 230.82(6). Requires utility written approval — not permitted by all utilities. Shifts sizing requirements to service entrance equipment and conductors. Switch to Supply-Side mode above for full requirements.

Option 4 — Main Breaker Reduction

If the main breaker is oversized relative to actual service capacity, reducing main breaker size increases available backfeed headroom.
Example: 200A bus / 200A main = 0A available.
         200A bus / 175A main = 65A available.
Requires utility approval. Do not downsize without written utility authorization.

Option 5 — Main Panel Upgrade (MPU)

Full panel replacement with higher rated busbar. Most common solution when service is undersized for both home load and solar addition.

MPU Triggers Bring-to-Code Requirements

Most jurisdictions require electrical work associated with an MPU to bring affected circuits to current code. Common requirements triggered by MPU permit:

  • NEC 210.12 [2023] — AFCI protection on bedroom, living room, kitchen, hallway, and dining room circuits.
  • NEC 210.8 [2023] — GFCI updates in all required locations — kitchen, bath, garage, outdoor, basement, crawl space, boathouse, accessory dwelling.
  • NEC 250.50 [2023] — Grounding electrode system — all electrodes present at structure must be bonded.
  • NEC 250.53 [2023] — Two ground rods required unless single rod meets 25 ohm resistance.
  • Service entrance conductor sizing per NEC 230.31.
  • Meter base upgrade if existing is not rated for new service size.
  • Smoke and CO detector requirements may be triggered by local amendment — confirm with AHJ.
From the Field — AHJ interpretation varies significantly. Some inspectors treat any panel upgrade as a full service upgrade triggering all of the above. Others scope requirements to affected circuits only. Confirm scope with AHJ before quoting the customer — an MPU can range from a straightforward panel swap to a significant whole-home electrical update depending on jurisdiction.
From the Field — NEC 230.79(C) requires 100A minimum service for single family dwellings. Services below 100A predate the 1978 NEC minimum. Any MPU must bring service to 100A minimum. Many AHJs and utilities require 200A minimum for new construction and solar installations.
60A Service Flag — 60A services are common in pre-1978 construction. Solar addition to a 60A service almost always requires an MPU. A 60A bus with 60A main: 60 × 1.2 = 72A, minus 60A main = only 12A available backfeed. Insufficient for virtually any modern inverter output.

Reference & Edge Cases

An alternative to the 120% rule. The total of all OCPD ratings on the busbar, including the proposed backfeed breaker, must not exceed the busbar rating. No 120% multiplier applies.

  • Panel fails the 120% rule but total load OCPD is low — the sum method may pass without a panel upgrade.
  • AHJ prefers total load enumeration over the percentage-based method.
  • Multiple interactive sources (PV + ESS + generator) where aggregate OCPD is easier to document.
  • Confirming a passing 120% result via an alternate method for stronger permit documentation.
From the Field — Interpretation varies by AHJ. Confirm acceptance before designing to this method. Some utilities apply more restrictive rules than the NEC minimum.

From the Field — Labeling is the most common reason solar inspections fail on the electrical side. Breaker lock, label size, placement, and exact wording are each individually checked. If multiple inverters are installed, each point of connection requires its own independent label set. Confirm local AHJ label format and size requirements before scheduling final inspection.

NEC 705.12 sets the minimum code standard for load-side interconnection. Utility Interconnection Agreements (IA) and tariff schedules are contractual and often impose stricter limits — they take precedence in practice regardless of NEC compliance.

Common utility restrictions that override NEC minimums:
  • Export capacity cap lower than NEC maximum (e.g., utility limits export to 15A regardless of panel capacity)
  • Anti-islanding delay requirements beyond IEEE 1547 minimums
  • Prohibition on specific inverter types, firmware versions, or rapid shutdown configurations
  • Metering requirements that restrict installation location or service entrance disconnect position
  • NEM/NEM3 queue restrictions that affect when a system can be energized
  • Minimum interconnection study thresholds that trigger additional engineering review
From the Field — Always obtain and review the utility IA before submitting permit documents. AHJ approval does not substitute for utility approval — both are required.

Rapid Shutdown — NEC 690.12

Rapid shutdown requirements vary significantly by NEC version. No calculation required — this is a system design and equipment selection requirement.

2014 / 2017

Conductors outside the array boundary must de-energize within 10 seconds of rapid shutdown initiation. Array conductors within 10 feet of a penetration point must also de-energize.

2020 / 2023

Controlled conductors within the array boundary must de-energize to ≤30V and ≤240VA within 30 seconds. Requires module-level electronics (MLPEs) or listed array energy shutdown equipment within the array boundary.

NEC 690.12(B)(2) [2023] — Controlled conductors: ≤30V / ≤240VA within 30 seconds of initiation. Most microinverter systems (Enphase IQ series) satisfy 2023 requirements by design. String inverter systems require a rapid shutdown device at each module or string. Confirm compliance method in equipment listing and AHJ accepted compliance path.
NEC 705.13 — Power Control System Exception

Introduced in NEC 2023. A Power Control System (PCS) manages energy sources in real time to ensure the total current fed into the busbar never exceeds the panel's rated capacity. When a listed PCS is installed and programmed to enforce this limit, the 120% busbar calculation under NEC 705.12 is not required.

NEC 705.13 Requirements
  • PCS must be a listed system specifically identified for the purpose.
  • PCS must actively limit combined backfeed to prevent busbar overcurrent — not just monitor or alarm.
  • Documentation of PCS programming and setpoints must be available for AHJ inspection.
  • If the PCS fails or loses control, the system must default to a safe state (typically de-energize or limit to zero export).
  • Supply-side interconnection rules still apply if the system has a supply-side connection.
Common PCS-Capable Systems
Tesla Gateway 3

Included with Powerwall 3. Controls solar inverter and battery output. Can be configured to enforce export limits. Listed for NEC 705.13 compliance in applicable configurations.

Enphase IQ System Controller 3

Manages Enphase microinverter and IQ Battery systems. Export limiting via Envoy communication. Confirm current listing status and AHJ acceptance for 705.13 pathway.

From the Field — The PCS pathway is relatively new and AHJ acceptance varies widely. Many inspectors are not yet familiar with 705.13 and may default to requiring the 120% calc regardless. Confirm with AHJ before designing to this pathway. Get AHJ acknowledgment in writing before submitting permit documents that rely on the PCS exception.
