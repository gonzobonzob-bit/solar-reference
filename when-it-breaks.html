By the Numbers

Solar underperformance is not rare and the causes are not random. Industry data points to consistent failure patterns — and consistent gaps between what systems are expected to produce and what they actually do.

39%
Inverters are the leading equipment cause of solar power losses in the United States — ahead of strings at 22% and combiners at 19%.
$10B
In global solar asset underperformance losses in 2024. Per-MW losses climbed from $977 in 2020 to $5,720 in 2024 — a nearly 6× increase in four years.
60%
Power losses at individual solar facilities during extreme weather events can reach up to 60% — short-term, but increasingly common.

From the Field

What To Look For

Before you call anyone, run through these. Most solar problems leave evidence — you just have to know where to look. These are the first four questions worth asking yourself whenever something feels off.

  • Is your monitoring showing actual production vs. expected? Every monitoring platform shows some form of expected vs. actual. If production is consistently below the estimate — not just on cloudy days — that gap is worth investigating.
  • When did you last check your inverter error log? Inverters log faults, warnings, and communication dropouts. Most homeowners never look at this. It's often the first place a developing problem shows up, sometimes weeks before production drops noticeably.
  • Has anything changed near your array? New trees, new neighboring structures, a chimney extension, new rooftop equipment — anything that casts a new shadow is worth checking. Shade that wasn't there at installation changes the performance picture significantly.
  • When was your last physical inspection? Connections loosen. Critters nest. Racking shifts in heavy wind and freeze-thaw cycles. A visual inspection once a year — or after any significant weather event — catches things monitoring cannot.
